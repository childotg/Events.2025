→ Titles, headcount, budget

In today's hybrid world, formal power matters less
than tangible contribution.

"Writing code doesn't reduce your authority—

Lesson 1

Understanding different
languages of value

Different Languages of Value

→ Product work: value = innovation and speed

→ Service work: value = reliability and client confidence

→ Engineers measure: performance, code elegance

→ Business measures: trust, margins, predictability

Service Company P&L

Revenue (Top Line) €10,000,000 100%
COGS (billable people + infra) €6,500,000 65%
Gross Margin €3,500,000 35%
OPEX (sales, admin, overhead) €2,500,000 25%
EBITDA €1,000,000 10%

Selling people + markup model

Product Company P&L

Revenue (Top Line) €10,000,000 100%
COGS (hosting, support, licenses) €1,500,000 15%
Gross Margin €8,500,000 85%
R&D (capitalized development) €3,500,000 35%
Sales & Marketing €2,500,000 25%
G&A (admin, overhead) €1,000,000 10%
EBITDA €1,500,000 15%

High gross margin + R&D investment + scalability

Two Different Economic Models

Service Company

• Gross Margin: 35%
• EBITDA: 10%
• COGS = people costs
• Linear scaling
• Margin focus

Product Company

• Gross Margin: 85%
• EBITDA: 15%
• COGS = infrastructure
• Exponential scaling
• Growth focus

As a technologist, understanding both models changes how you communicate value

"You don't have to abandon
the technical language—

you have to translate it."

"Empathy is not agreement;
it's understanding."

Building the Bridge

For technologists, empathy isn't asking the business to understand our systems.

It's building the bridge in their language
—even when they don't speak ours.

An act of humility, not submission

"Good leaders must first become
good servants."

— Robert Greenleaf

Example: Speaking Their Language

Technical Message

"We refactored the authentication module for better code maintainability and reduced technical debt"

Business Message

"We cut client onboarding time from 3 days to 4 hours—faster time to sign new contracts"

Same work, different language

Humility as a Practice

Humility doesn't mean hiding your skill—

it means using it to understand others better.

It's the antidote to technical arrogance:

Not having the last word,
but asking the right first question.

Micro-practices

→ Build one PoC per quarter

→ Review code on strategic projects

→ Shadow teams that don't report to you

Small actions, sustained credibility

"Stop building,
and your technical reputation
fades fast."

Technical credibility is a battery—
recharge it in the field

Lesson 3

Using small wins as
strategic leverage

"The best way to influence
without authority is through
concrete examples
that speak for themselves."

Proof-of-concepts are the modern form
of technical persuasion

Small Wins in Action

→ A small script that links two internal systems

→ A shared dashboard connecting technical and business metrics

→ An automation that removes repetitive manual work

Start small, prove value, scale impact

The Trojan-horse PoC

→ A mini-project solving a real, visible problem

→ Measurable impact, minimal cost

→ Built to show potential, not perfection

Practical Examples

Bridging business units
Simple API connecting two isolated databases
→ Later adopted by both teams

Talking with the board
Dashboard linking uptime or bug data to revenue and NPS
→ Makes technical work visible to leadership

Reducing internal friction
Automation script removing repetitive manual work
→ Frees time across departments

"Each PoC becomes a
common language.

It doesn't explain—it shows.

It's the most credible
form of strategy."

How to Build It

1. Pick the right problem
Affects two functions, has a natural sponsor

2. Limit the scope
One week of work maximum

3. Measure the impact
Show before and after

4. Frame as opportunity
Not challenge, but possibility

"Create new leaders,
make yourself unnecessary."

Putting It All Together

We've covered three ideas:

→ Maintaining credibility as an individual contributor

Three Steps to Influence Without Authority

3. Build alliances through delivery
Don't persuade—collaborate
